## What Topics Count as Ethics for Wisconsin Professional Engineers?

Qualifying topics include the NSPE Code of Ethics, the duty to the public, conflicts of interest, confidentiality, and professional responsibility in design. A professional engineer ethics course addressing these areas generally satisfies DSPS requirements. Content from NSPE, state boards, or accredited providers like PDH Pro typically qualifies. Ethics courses should delve into real-world applications that challenge engineers to think critically about ethical dilemmas they may face in practice.

To avoid missteps, ensure your courses have a clear focus on professional engineering ethics and can be backed up with sufficient evidence of completion and relevance.

## How the Wisconsin PE Ethics Requirement Fits into the 30-PDH Rule

Wisconsin ties its ethics requirement directly to the broader continuing education rules for Professional Engineers. The state requires you to complete a total of **30 Professional Development Hours (PDHs)** every two-year renewal period, and ethics is one defined part of that total.

Within those 30 PDHs:

- You must complete **a minimum of 2 PDHs in professional conduct and ethics**.
- You must also complete **at least 13 PDHs in live, real-time interactive formats**, such as webinars or in-person courses where you can interact with the instructor during the session.

The ethics hours are not “extra” on top of the 30; they are included within the 30-PDH total. You are free to take more than 2 hours of ethics if you find a topic particularly relevant or valuable, but only 2 hours are specifically mandated as ethics content. Any additional ethics hours simply count toward your 30-hour requirement.

The **continuing education biennium** for Wisconsin PEs runs from **August 1 of even-numbered years through July 31 of the next even-numbered year**. Your 30 PDHs, including the 2 ethics hours and the 13 live hours, must be completed within that window to apply to the current renewal cycle.

## What Counts as Ethics for Wisconsin PEs

Wisconsin’s rules describe the requirement as **“professional conduct and ethics”** but do not publish a detailed, official topic list. Instead, the standard is that the content must clearly relate to professional engineering practice and ethical responsibilities.

In practical terms, ethics content typically includes areas such as:

- The engineer’s **duty to protect public health, safety, and welfare**
- The **NSPE Code of Ethics for Engineers** and how it applies in real-world situations
- **Conflicts of interest**, including how to identify, avoid, and disclose them
- **Sealing, signing, and responsible charge** obligations
- **Wisconsin-specific laws and rules** that govern engineering practice and discipline
- **Professional responsibility in design**, documentation, and communication
- Handling **client and employer pressures** that conflict with ethical standards
- **Whistleblowing**, reporting unsafe conditions, and responding to misconduct

Any course you choose for the ethics requirement should clearly connect these kinds of issues to the day-to-day work of a Professional Engineer. If the course description and learning objectives emphasize engineering practice, decision-making, and professional obligations, it is more likely to be accepted as ethics content.

On the other hand, some topics are risky or weak candidates for your required ethics hours:

- General corporate compliance or HR-style training (such as generic workplace harassment modules) that does not clearly tie to engineering responsibilities
- Business ethics courses that discuss broad corporate dilemmas but never address engineering practice, licensure, or duty to the public
- Unstructured self-study (for example, reading articles or watching random videos) that does not provide a syllabus, defined objectives, or documentation of completion

These types of activities might be useful for your general development, but they are harder to justify as meeting Wisconsin’s **professional conduct and ethics** requirement because they lack a direct link to engineering practice or cannot be documented clearly if you are audited.

## Provider Approval, Course Selection, and Your Responsibility

One of the most important points to understand is that **Wisconsin does not pre-approve courses, providers, or activities for PE continuing education**. The Professional Engineer Section explicitly states that it does not maintain any approved provider list and reserves the final say on whether any particular course meets the rules.

That means:

- You are personally responsible for selecting courses that meet Wisconsin’s standards.
- Even if a course is marketed as “ethics for Professional Engineers” or “designed to meet Wisconsin requirements,” that is not an official state endorsement.
- The Board can later decide that a course does not qualify if it does not sufficiently relate to professional engineering practice or ethics.

To reduce your risk, look for ethics offerings that:

- Are clearly labeled for engineers or licensed professionals
- Reference engineering codes of ethics, state board rules, or professional responsibility
- Provide clear learning objectives that relate to practice, risk, and duty to the public
- Offer a certificate of completion specifying the course title, provider, date, and PDH hours

Many Wisconsin PEs use ethics courses from professional societies, national engineering organizations, or reputable continuing education providers. These can be very good options, but they still fall under the same rule: **you** must make sure the content fits Wisconsin’s definition of professional conduct and ethics, and the Board retains final discretion.

## Live vs. Online Hours: How Ethics Fits In

Wisconsin’s rules do more than just require ethics; they also shape how you earn your hours across different formats. Out of the 30 total PDHs:

- At least 13 PDHs must come from **live, real-time interactive courses**.
- The remaining hours can be completed through other acceptable methods, such as on-demand online courses or self-paced learning, provided they meet the content and documentation requirements.

In practice, your 2 ethics hours can be:

- Live ethics webinars or in-person seminars that qualify toward the 13 required live PDHs, or
- On-demand or self-paced ethics courses that count toward your non-live PDHs, as long as they clearly qualify as professional conduct and ethics content.

The key is to plan your biennium so you do not end up short in either category. A good approach is to assign at least one of your ethics courses to the live category so you are meeting both requirements at once.

## Recordkeeping, Audits, and How Long to Keep Your Ethics Certificates

Wisconsin uses an audit-style system rather than requiring you to submit documentation upfront. When you renew, you typically certify that you have met the continuing education requirements, but you **do not send certificates automatically**.

However, the Department and the Professional Engineer Section can request your records at any time within the retention period. If that happens, you need to be able to show:

- A record of each course or activity, including the **course title, provider, date, and PDH hours**
- Documentation of completion, such as **certificates, transcripts, or letters** from the provider
- Enough detail to show that ethics courses truly addressed **professional conduct and ethics** within the context of engineering practice

By rule, you must maintain your continuing education records **for at least the three most recent biennia**. In simple terms, that means you should be able to document about six years’ worth of CE history at any given time. If you are ever audited, gaps in your records or vague course descriptions can create problems, even if you genuinely completed the hours.

The safest habit is to:

- Save digital copies of all certificates in a structured folder by biennium
- Maintain a simple tracking sheet listing each course, provider, date, PDHs, and whether it was ethics or technical
- Cross-check at the end of the biennium that you have at least 30 total PDHs, at least 2 PDHs in professional conduct and ethics, and at least 13 PDHs in live, real-time interactive formats, all within the correct date window

Treat your CE documentation with the same care you would give to project records. It is much easier to keep clean records as you go than to reconstruct them years later under audit pressure.
